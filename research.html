Taking Sides: Party Competition, Interest Group Strategy, and the Polarization of American Pluralism

Jesse M. Crosson, Alexander C. Furnas, and Geoffrey M. Lorenz

Book manuscript in preparation ¬∑ Download proposal ‚Üó

About the book

While most interest groups in the United States still describe themselves as non-partisan, they have, in practice, aligned themselves with one of the two major political parties. Taking Sides documents a 50-year growth in interest group partisanship, examines why and when groups have grown more partisan, and explores the consequences for American government and democracy.

Leveraging a massive new dataset of over 200,000 interest group positions on congressional legislation from 1973 to 2021, along with innovative preference scaling and text analysis, the book offers the first examination of how modern party competition influences interest group strategy and lobbying success. We argue that party competition ‚Äî rooted in the rise of insecure majorities and solidified during the Gingrich Revolution ‚Äî has ensnared many organized interests into a feedback loop in which partisanship gets groups' priorities onto the legislative agenda, but only when the group contributes to their party's political brand. The result is a new democratic challenge: once drawn into partisan coalitions, interest groups no longer operate as independent advocates for their members' priorities, but become extensions of party networks ‚Äî eroding the fluid, cross-cutting coalitions that pluralist theories of democracy assume.
